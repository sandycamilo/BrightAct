Domestic Violence

Domestic violence is a constant concern. The people it hurts often carry emotional wounds for a lifetime.

Unless otherwise noted, all data is for EU member states

in the EU
has experienced physical and/or sexual violence
since she was 15 years old *

women have experienced physical/sexual violence in the course of 12 months before the survey interviews.

Differences in EU States

Gender equality

could lead to higher levels of disclosure about violence against women. Incidents of violence against women are more likely to be openly addressed and challenged in societies with greater equality.

exposure to violence

Women’s exposure to risk factors for violence can be examined at the Member State level with respect to factors that might increase exposure to violence These include patterns in employment (working outside the home), as well as socialisation and lifestyle patterns (going out and dating).

drinking habits

There is evidence of the relationship between perpetrators’ drinking habits and women’s experiences of domestic violence. Different drinking patterns in Member States may help to explain certain aspects of violence against women, which, in turn, need to be looked at alongside patterns in individual perpetrators’ violent behaviour, which may not be limited to violence against women.

urbanisation

Differences between countries in overall levels of violent crime need to be looked at alongside findings on violence against women. Urbanisation in a Member State is generally related to higher crime rates.

private matter

in societies in which intimate partner violence is considered largely a private matter incidents of violence against women are unlikely to be shared with family and friends and are also rarely reported to the police. Such reticence may inhibit talking to survey interviewers.

We must act urgently so save lives during COVID-19

The shadow pandemic - There has been a significant escalation of domestic violence worldwide.
We see a shadow pandemic growing, of domestic violence by an intimate partner or family member. As more countries report infection and lockdown, more domestic violence helplines and shelters accross the world are reporting rising calls for help.

Even before COVID-19, domestic violence was one of the greatest human rights violations. As the COVID-19 pandemic continues, the numbed of victims is likey to grow with mutiple impacts on women's wellbeing, sexual and reproductive health, mental health, and ability to participate and lead in the recovery of our sociaties and economy.
Confinement is fostering the tension and strain created by security, health, and money worries. And it is increasing isolation for victims with violent partners, separating them from the people, and resources that can best help them. It's a perfect storm for controlling, violent behaviour behind closed doors.

And in parallel, as health systems are stretching to breaking point, domestic violence shelters are also reaching capacity, a service deficit made worse when centres are repurposed for additional COVID-response.

We are awarded by the EU Innovation Council as: "A scalable solution to save lives during COVID-19."
